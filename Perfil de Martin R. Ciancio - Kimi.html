Un "white paper" en el contexto científico es un documento breve y persuasivo que presenta un problema, propone una solución innovadora y busca generar interés/apoyo para un proyecto. 📄 ¿Qué es un White Paper para SVP?

Propósito

  • Presentar una idea de proyecto emergente ante la comunidad internacional de paleontólogos de vertebrados
  • Buscar colaboradores, feedback crítico y visibilidad antes de escribir una propuesta formal de fondos
  • Posicionarte como líder de opinión en un tema novedoso (Evo-Devo en Xenarthra)

  • El congreso anual de SVP (~1,200-1,500 asistentes de todo el mundo) es el evento más importante de paleontología de vertebrados
  • Muchos programas de financiamiento (NSF, ERC) valoran que hayas "testeado" tu idea en este foro
  • Es común que en las sesiones de pósters o "round tables" se presenten white papers para iniciar discusiones

1. THE GAP: A Missing Link in Mammalian Evolution

The Cingulata (armadillos and glyptodonts) represent one of the most ecologically diverse and long-lived radiations among South American mammals, spanning over 50 million years and encompassing forms ranging from small burrowing insectivores to multi-ton herbivorous giants. Despite their dominance in Cenozoic ecosystems and their status as the only xenarthran group with extant representatives, the evolutionary developmental mechanisms driving their morphological diversification remain virtually unexplored.
Current research on Cingulata has focused primarily on systematics, biochronology, and general paleoecology. However, we lack an integrative framework connecting the fossil record with biological processes—specifically, how developmental changes in their unique dermal armor (osteoderms) facilitated adaptive shifts in body size, locomotion, and feeding ecology. This gap represents a critical blind spot in vertebrate paleontology: while Evo-Devo (evolutionary developmental biology) has revolutionized our understanding of other mammalian clades (notably cetaceans and ungulates), xenarthrans remain "black boxes" due to their specialized anatomy and perceived phylogenetic isolation.
The central question: How did modular evolution of osteoderms enable the Cingulata to colonize disparate ecological niches, from fossorial insectivory to bulk-feeding gigantism?

2. THE OPPORTUNITY: Why Now?

Three converging factors make this project feasible and urgent:
Technological readiness: High-resolution computed tomography (μCT), 3D morphometrics, and finite element analysis (FEA) have matured sufficiently to model biomechanical performance of complex biological structures. Simultaneously, transcriptomic and genomic tools can now be applied to developmental questions in non-model organisms, including Dasypus novemcinctus (whose genome was sequenced in 2022).
Collections availability: Argentine institutions (Museo de La Plata, MEF Trelew, MACN) house exceptional Cingulata fossils spanning the entire Cenozoic, including the most complete Paleogene records globally. Additionally, Miocene faunas from Bolivia (Cerdas, Quebrada Honda) and Colombia (La Venta) provide crucial intermediate morphologies.
Theoretical framework: Recent advances in "total evidence" phylogenetics and Bayesian morphological clock methods allow robust integration of extant and extinct taxa. The concept of "modularity" in evolutionary biology provides a testable hypothesis: osteoderms, as serially repeated structures with semi-independent genetic regulation, may have served as evolutionary modules facilitating rapid phenotypic shifts.

3. THE APPROACH: An Integrated Research Program

We propose a 4-year international collaborative project combining four methodological pillars:

Pillar 1: Phylogenetic Total Evidence

Generate the first species-level phylogeny of Cingulata integrating:
  • 3D geometric morphometrics of cranial and postcranial elements (500+ specimens)
  • Discrete morphological characters from fossil and extant taxa
  • Molecular data from extant species (mitochondrial and nuclear loci)
  • Bayesian "fossilized birth-death" dating to reconstruct tempo of diversification

Pillar 2: Paleo-Evo-Devo of Osteoderms

Test the hypothesis that osteoderm modularity drove adaptive radiation through:
  • Histological sectioning of fossil osteoderms (thin-section petrography, SEM)
  • Comparative embryology of extant armadillos (Dasypus, Euphractus, Chaetophractus) documenting ossification sequences
  • Transcriptomic analysis of developing dermal tissue in extant taxa (collaboration with developmental biologists)
  • 3D microstructure reconstruction using synchrotron μCT to visualize vascularization and fiber orientation

Pillar 3: Biomechanical Modeling

Use FEA to test functional hypotheses:
  • Mechanical performance of different osteoderm morphologies under compressive and shear loads
  • Correlation between osteoderm thickness/density and inferred body mass in glyptodonts
  • Simulation of predator-prey interactions (e.g., saber-tooth cat bites on glyptodont armor)

Pillar 4: Paleoecological Context

Isotopic and taphonomic analysis to correlate morphological changes with environmental shifts:
  • Stable isotopes (δ13C, δ18O) from osteoderm carbonate to reconstruct diet and climate
  • Taphonomic bias correction for body-size trends in the fossil record

4. THE IMPACT: Significance for Vertebrate Paleontology

This project addresses three major challenges in our discipline:
1. Integrating paleontology with Evo-Devo: While developmental biology has transformed neontological systematics, paleontology has struggled to incorporate developmental data. Osteoderms offer a rare opportunity because they preserve developmental information (growth rings, remodeling patterns) and have direct ontogenetic analogs in extant species.
2. Understanding adaptive radiations: Cingulata exemplify a classic "explosive radiation" following ecological release (post-Cretaceous-Paleogene boundary). Identifying the developmental genetic architecture underlying this radiation provides a model applicable to other mammalian groups.
3. Open science infrastructure: We will generate the first open-access 3D repository of Cingulata fossils (MorphoSource platform), including 200+ μCT datasets and interactive 3D models for education and research. This addresses persistent inequities in access to South American paleontological collections.

¿Cómo usar este código?

Paso 1: Guardar el archivo

  1. Copiá todo el código de arriba
  2. Abrí un editor de texto (Notepad, TextEdit, VS Code)
  3. Pegá el código y guardalo como index.html

Paso 2: Subir a GitHub Pages (GRATIS)

  1. Creá una cuenta en github.com
  2. Creá un nuevo repositorio llamado martinciancio.github.io
  3. Subí el archivo index.html
  4. En Settings > Pages, seleccioná "Deploy from branch" > main
  5. ¡Listo! Tu sitio estará en https://martinciancio.github.io

Paso 3: Personalizar

  • Tu foto: Reemplazá el 👨‍🔬 en la sección "About" con <img src="tu-foto.jpg" alt="Martín R. Ciancio">
  • Infografía: Insertá la imagen que generamos en la sección "Featured Project"
  • Publicaciones: Actualizá la lista con tus papers reales
  • Colores: Modificá las variables CSS en :root si querés cambiar el look
